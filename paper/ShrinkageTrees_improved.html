ShrinkageTrees: An R Package for Bayesian Tree Ensembles for Survival Analysis and Causal Inference

ShrinkageTrees is an R package for fitting Bayesian tree ensemble models with flexible regularisation mechanisms for survival analysis and causal inference. The package provides unified implementations of BART, DART, and the \(\tau\)-learner (Bayesian Causal Forests) for right-censored and interval-censored survival outcomes via accelerated failure time (AFT) formulations. Multiple regularisation strategies can be combined within a single model: structural sparsity through tree depth priors and Dirichlet splitting priors (DART), and parametric shrinkage on the leaf step heights through global–local scale mixture priors. A key contribution is the implementation of Horseshoe Forests, which place a horseshoe prior directly on the step heights, enabling adaptive shrinkage that aggressively regularises uninformative leaves while preserving strong signals — a strategy particularly suited to high-dimensional settings. An efficient Rcpp backend and coherent S3 interface support reproducible workflows for both prediction and causal effect estimation.

1 Introduction

Bayesian Additive Regression Trees [BART; Chipman et al. (2010)] model the response as a sum of \(m\) shallow decision trees, each regularised through a Bayesian prior. BART captures complex interactions and non-linearities without specifying a parametric form. This makes BART well suited to settings where the relationship between covariates and the response is unknown, including survival analysis. The BART package (Sparapani et al. 2021) is, to our knowledge, the only CRAN package that implements Bayesian tree ensembles for survival data. It supports discrete-time hazard and AFT formulations, competing risks, and recurrent events. However, it does not support interval-censored outcomes, causal inference models, or global–local shrinkage priors on the leaf parameters. We address all three limitations in ShrinkageTrees.

BART has become a popular tool for causal inference (Hill 2011; Dorie et al. 2019). Hahn et al. (2020) propose Bayesian Causal Forests (BCF), which decompose the response surface into a prognostic function and a treatment effect function; Caron et al. (2022) later term this the \(\tau\)-learner. This separation improves estimation of heterogeneous treatment effects. The bcf and stochtree packages implement the \(\tau\)-learner for continuous outcomes. Causal inference and survival analysis arise together naturally in clinical trials and observational studies, where the outcome is a time-to-event and treatment effects may vary across patient subgroups. No existing R package implements the \(\tau\)-learner for survival endpoints.

High-dimensional settings, where the number of predictors \(p\) is large relative to the sample size \(n\), demand adequate regularisation. We distinguish two components on which regularisation can act in Bayesian tree ensembles. The first is the tree structure, which determines how the covariate space is partitioned. The second is the leaf parameters, which determine each partition’s contribution to the fitted function. Standard BART regularises both components generically: a depth prior discourages complex trees, and a conjugate normal prior shrinks the leaf parameters. DART (Linero 2018) strengthens structural regularisation by placing a Dirichlet prior on the splitting probabilities, encouraging the model to split on a smaller subset of relevant predictors. Jacobs et al. (2025) target the second component and place global–local shrinkage priors, such as the horseshoe (Carvalho et al. 2010), directly on the leaf parameters. This shrinks weak or spurious contributions aggressively toward zero while preserving strong signals. We refer to tree ensembles with such priors as Horseshoe Forests.

We introduce ShrinkageTrees, a unified R package that addresses the three gaps identified above. We extend BART to survival outcomes via AFT models for both right-censored and interval-censored data. We extend the \(\tau\)-learner to survival endpoints, providing, to our knowledge, the first causal forest implementation for time-to-event outcomes in R. We implement both Dirichlet splitting priors and Horseshoe Forests, allowing both forms of regularisation to be combined freely within a single model. The Horseshoe Forest sampler relies on an efficient reversible jump algorithm implemented in C++ via Rcpp (Eddelbuettel and François 2011), and is designed to be extendable to other global–local shrinkage priors. We provide a coherent S3 interface with , , , and methods. Jacobs et al. (2025) develop the methodological foundations.

To give a first impression of the package interface, a Horseshoe Forest for right-censored survival data can be fitted in a single call:

library(ShrinkageTrees)
data("ovarian")
fit <- HorseTrees(y = time, status = status, X_train = X,
                  outcome_type = "right-censored", number_of_trees = 200)

Section 3 reviews the statistical methodology behind the models. Section 4 returns to the example and progressively adds horseshoe shrinkage, causal inference, and interval-censored outcomes. Section 5 describes the package architecture. Section 6 compares ShrinkageTrees with related R packages. Section 7 concludes.

2 A first example: survival prediction

We introduce the package through a complete worked example before discussing the underlying methodology. We use the dataset shipped with ShrinkageTrees, curated from The Cancer Genome Atlas ovarian cancer cohort [TCGA-OV; Cancer Genome Atlas Research Network (2011)]. The dataset contains overall survival times, a binary treatment indicator (carboplatin vs. cisplatin), clinical covariates, and expression levels of 2,000 genes selected by median absolute deviation. Ovarian cancer is one of the most lethal gynaecological malignancies, with a five-year survival rate below 50%. Gene expression patterns are known to be associated with survival duration and chemotherapy response (Cancer Genome Atlas Research Network 2011). The dataset provides a challenging high-dimensional setting (\(p \gg n\)) that exercises the main features of the package.

library(ShrinkageTrees)
set.seed(42)
data("ovarian")

clin      <- ovarian$clinical
time      <- clin$OS_time / 30.44   # convert days to months
status    <- clin$OS_event
treatment <- clin$treatment

X <- cbind(age        = clin$age,
           figo_stage = clin$figo_stage,
           tumor_grade = clin$tumor_grade,
           ovarian$X)

The dataset comprises 357 patients with 2,000 gene expression features and three clinical covariates (age, FIGO stage, tumour grade). We hold out 20% of the patients for evaluating predictive accuracy.

train_idx <- sample(seq_len(nrow(clin)), size = floor(0.8 * nrow(clin)))
test_idx  <- setdiff(seq_len(nrow(clin)), train_idx)

X_train <- X[train_idx, ];  X_test <- X[test_idx, ]
time_train <- time[train_idx];  time_test <- time[test_idx]
status_train <- status[train_idx];  status_test <- status[test_idx]

We begin with the simplest model available in ShrinkageTrees: a standard BART survival model via SurvivalBART(). This uses an accelerated failure time (AFT) formulation with right-censoring and a conjugate normal prior on the leaf step heights — the classical BART specification of Chipman et al. (2010) applied to survival data.

  timescale       = "time",
  number_of_trees = 200,
  N_post          = 5000,
  N_burn          = 5000,
  n_chains        = 4
)

This standard BART model serves as a baseline. In Section 4 we return to this dataset and show how horseshoe shrinkage and causal inference models can improve upon and extend this analysis. First, we review the statistical methodology that underpins these extensions.

3 Statistical methodology

We build on three components. First, we review the BART model and the AFT formulation for survival outcomes. Second, we show how regularisation targets either the tree structures or the step heights — and how both strategies can be combined. Third, we extend the framework to causal inference via the \(\tau\)-learner.

3.1 BART and the accelerated failure time model

We adapt the BART model of Chipman et al. (2010). BART models the response as a sum of \(m\) shallow decision trees: \[ Y_i = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2). \] Each tree \(j\) is characterised by two parameter groups. The tree structure \(\mathcal{T}_j\) encodes the topology and the splitting rules: which variables to split on and at what thresholds. It determines how the covariate space is partitioned into disjoint regions. The step heights \(\mathcal{H}_j = \{h_{j1}, \ldots, h_{jL_j}\}\) are the values assigned to the \(L_j\) leaves and determine each region’s contribution to the fitted function. For input \(\mathbf{x}\), the function \(g(\mathbf{x};\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j)\) returns the step height of the leaf that \(\mathbf{x}\) reaches (see Figure 1). This distinction between \(\mathcal{T}\) and \(\mathcal{H}\) is central to the regularisation framework we develop below.

Figure 1: Schematic of a single regression tree. Interior nodes contain binary splitting rules of the form \(x_\rho < c\); terminal nodes (leaves) contain step heights \(h_\ell\). An observation traverses the tree from root to leaf, and its prediction is the step height of the leaf it reaches.

We specify independent priors on \(\mathcal{T}_j\) and \(\mathcal{H}_j\): \[ p(\mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) = p_{\mathcal{T}}(\mathcal{T}_j)\, p_{\mathcal{H}}(\mathcal{H}_j \mid \mathcal{T}_j) \quad \text{for } j = 1,\ldots,m. \] The tree structure prior \(p_{\mathcal{T}}\) is a branching process (Chipman et al. 1998; Ročková and Saha 2020) in which a node at depth \(d\) splits with probability \(\alpha\, (1 + d)^{-\beta}\). At each internal node, the splitting variable is drawn uniformly from the available covariates and the split point uniformly from the observed range.

The step height prior \(p_{\mathcal{H}}\) assigns independent Gaussian priors conditional on the tree structure: \[ h_{j\ell} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \frac{1}{4\sqrt{m}}\right), \] where the variance is scaled so that the prior places approximately 95% of its mass within the observed range of the response. This prior is conjugate, so the Gibbs sampler can update the step heights efficiently.

We model the error variance as \(\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-}\chi^2(\nu,\, \psi)\), with \(\nu = 3\) and \(\psi\) chosen so that the prior is centred around a rough estimate of \(\sigma^2\) (Chipman et al. 2010).

For survival outcomes, we adopt an accelerated failure time (AFT) formulation. Let \(T_i\) denote the event time, \(C_i\) the censoring time, \(Y_i = \min(T_i, C_i)\) the observed follow-up time, and \(\delta_i \in \{0, 1\}\) the event indicator. The prediction model becomes \[ \log T_i = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, T_j, H_j) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2). \] Right-censored observations (\(\delta_i = 0\)) are handled by imputing \(\log T_i\) from a truncated normal distribution within the Gibbs sampler. ShrinkageTrees also supports interval-censored outcomes, where the event time is known only to lie in an interval \((l_i, r_i]\); these are imputed analogously. This is the model behind the SurvivalBART() call in Section 2.

3.2 Regularisation

The parameters of a BART ensemble fall into two groups: tree structure parameters (nodes, splitting variables, split points) that determine how the covariate space is partitioned, and step heights that determine each partition’s contribution to the fitted function. Regularisation can target either group.

Structural regularisation. Standard BART regularises the tree structure through the branching process prior of Chipman et al. (2010): each internal node at depth \(d\) splits with probability \(\alpha (1 + d)^{-\beta}\), where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) and \(\beta \geq 0\) discourage deep trees. Under the default values \(\alpha = 0.95\) and \(\beta = 2\), most trees remain shallow. DART (Linero 2018) strengthens structural regularisation by replacing the uniform distribution over splitting variables with a Dirichlet prior on the splitting probabilities \(\mathbf{s} = (s_1, \ldots, s_p)\). By concentrating the prior on sparse probability vectors, DART encourages the ensemble to split on a subset of relevant variables, achieving structural variable selection.

Shrinkage on step heights. Rather than regularising the tree structure alone, we can place shrinkage priors directly on the leaf step heights. ShrinkageTrees adopts a scale mixture of normals representation, \[ h_\ell \mid \lambda_\ell, \tau, \omega \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \omega\, \lambda_\ell^2\, \tau^2), \] where \(\tau\) is a global shrinkage parameter shared across all leaves, \(\lambda_\ell\) is a local scale specific to leaf \(\ell\), and \(\omega\) is a fixed scaling constant. The global parameter \(\tau\) controls the overall degree of regularisation, while the local parameters \(\lambda_\ell\) allow individual leaves with strong signals to escape shrinkage.

The horseshoe prior (Carvalho et al. 2010) arises as a specific instantiation of this framework by placing independent half-Cauchy priors on both the local and global scales: \[ \lambda_\ell \sim \text{C}^+(0, \alpha_\lambda), \qquad \tau \sim \text{C}^+(0, \alpha_\tau), \] where \(\text{C}^+(0, \alpha)\) denotes the half-Cauchy distribution with scale \(\alpha\). The half-Cauchy has its median at \(\alpha\) and possesses heavy tails — its mean and variance do not exist — which allows the local scales to take very large values when the data support a strong effect (Polson and Scott 2012). This combination aggressively shrinks the contributions of uninformative leaves towards zero while preserving large, data-supported signals. Following Jacobs et al. (2025), we set \(\alpha_\tau = \alpha_\lambda = k / \sqrt{m}\). Smaller values of \(k\) yield stronger shrinkage. We recommend \(k \in [0.05,\, 0.5]\) as a practical range; the default in ShrinkageTrees is \(k = 0.1\). When domain knowledge does not suggest a specific value, \(k\) can be selected via cross-validation. Jacobs et al. (2025) provide a sensitivity analysis. The Dirichlet and horseshoe priors address complementary aspects of regularisation — structural sparsity and parametric shrinkage, respectively — and can be combined within a single model in ShrinkageTrees.

3.3 The \(\tau\)-learner for causal inference

The BART formulation above estimates a single function of the covariates — we refer to this as the prediction model. The prediction models can themselves be used for causal inference: including the treatment indicator as a covariate yields an S-learner, while fitting separate models per treatment arm yields a T-learner (Caron et al. 2022). However, the dedicated approach in ShrinkageTrees is the \(\tau\)-learner, the decomposition proposed by Hahn et al. (2020) as Bayesian Causal Forests (BCF) and later termed the \(\tau\)-learner by Caron et al. (2022). Given a binary treatment indicator \(A_i \in \{0, 1\}\) and an estimate of the propensity score \(\hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \hat{P}(A_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)\), the causal model writes \[ y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)) + A_i\, \tau(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i, \] where \(\mu\) is a prognostic function modelled by a forest of \(m_\mu\) trees and \(\tau\) is a treatment effect function modelled by a separate forest of \(m_\tau\) trees. This separation allows the prognostic and treatment effect components to have different regularisation and complexity. The conditional average treatment effect is \(\text{CATE}(\mathbf{x}) = \tau(\mathbf{x})\), and the average treatment effect is \(\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[\tau(\mathbf{X})]\).

More generally, the scalar multiplier on \(\tau\) in the \(\tau\)-learner need not equal the raw treatment indicator. Writing \(b_i\) for the coding applied to subject \(i\), the model becomes \(y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)) + b_i\, \tau(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i\). Hahn et al. (2020) discuss several choices. Centered coding sets \(b_i = A_i - 1/2\), which is the original \(\tau\)-learner parameterisation and the default in ShrinkageTrees. Binary coding uses \(b_i = A_i\). Adaptive coding sets \(b_i = A_i - \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)\), incorporating the estimated propensity score directly into the treatment multiplier; this is the parameterisation adopted in the bcf package. Finally, the invariant (parameter-expanded) coding of Hahn et al. (2020 5.2) assigns group-specific intercepts \(b_0, b_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/2)\) and writes \(y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i) + b_{A_i}\, \tilde{\tau}(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i\), where \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) are updated within the Gibbs sampler via conjugate normal updates and the treatment effect is recovered as \(\tau(\mathbf{x}_i) = (b_1 - b_0)\, \tilde{\tau}(\mathbf{x}_i)\). This parameterisation is invariant to the labelling of treatment and control. All four options are available in ShrinkageTrees via the treatment_coding argument.

The \(\tau\)-learner extends directly to the AFT setting by replacing \(y_i\) with \(\log T_i\).

3.4 Posterior computation

All models are fitted using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The outer loop is a Gibbs sampler that cycles through updates of the tree topologies, the leaf step heights, the error variance \(\sigma^2\), and — where applicable — the shrinkage parameters and censored event times. Trees are updated one at a time via Bayesian backfitting (Chipman et al. 2010): at each sweep, a partial residual is formed by subtracting the contributions of all other trees from the response, and tree \(j\) is then updated conditional on the current state of all other trees. The tree topology is modified through Metropolis–Hastings proposals that either grow a terminal node into an internal node with two new leaves, or prune an internal node back to a leaf.

Under the standard BART and Dirichlet (DART) priors, where the leaf parameters have a conjugate normal prior, the marginal likelihood of a proposed tree can be computed in closed form and the step heights are integrated out analytically. When global–local shrinkage priors such as the horseshoe are placed on the step heights, this conjugacy is lost: the local and global scale parameters \(\lambda_\ell\) and \(\tau\) must be sampled explicitly, and the tree proposals become reversible jump MCMC moves. The details of this reversible jump construction are given in Jacobs et al. (2025).

4 Building up the example

We return to the ovarian cancer dataset from Section 2 and progressively demonstrate more advanced features of ShrinkageTrees: horseshoe shrinkage for improved regularisation, causal inference for treatment effect estimation, and interval-censored survival modelling.

4.1 Adding horseshoe shrinkage

We now fit a Horseshoe Forest via HorseTrees() to the same data. The horseshoe prior applies global–local shrinkage to the leaf step heights, providing stronger regularisation than the conjugate normal prior used by SurvivalBART() — particularly beneficial in this high-dimensional setting where the number of predictors far exceeds the sample size.

4.2 Causal inference: treatment effects for survival

We now estimate heterogeneous treatment effects of carboplatin versus cisplatin using CausalHorseForest(), which fits a \(\tau\)-learner with horseshoe shrinkage on both the prognostic and treatment effect forests. We first estimate propensity scores using a probit Horseshoe Forest on the clinical covariates. We choose a Horseshoe Forest over logistic regression because it handles potential non-linearities and interactions between clinical covariates without manual specification, while the horseshoe prior guards against overfitting in this low-sample-size setting. Using the full gene expression matrix for propensity estimation would risk overfitting given \(p \gg n\); clinical covariates alone capture the main sources of confounding by indication.

X_ps <- cbind(age = clin$age, figo_stage = clin$figo_stage,
              tumor_grade = clin$tumor_grade)
ps_fit <- HorseTrees(
  y               = treatment,

4.3 Interval-censored outcomes: a simulation study

We validate ShrinkageTrees on a simulated interval-censored survival dataset with known ground truth. The simulation targets the setting where the horseshoe prior is expected to provide the largest benefit: a sparse, high-dimensional covariate space with interval-censored outcomes.

Design

We generate \(n = 100\) observations with \(p = 500\) covariates drawn independently from \(\mathcal{N}(0, 1)\). Only the first 5 covariates are active. The true log-survival time follows \[ \log T_i = 2 + x_{i1} - 0.8\, x_{i2} + 0.6\, x_{i3} + 0.5\, x_{i1}\, x_{i4} - 0.4\, x_{i5} + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 0.5^2). \] We generate interval-censored observations by drawing inspection times \(V_{i1} < V_{i2} < V_{i3}\) uniformly over the support of \(T\) and recording the interval \((L_i, R_i]\) in which the event falls. Approximately \(\ldots\)% of observations are interval-censored, \(\ldots\)% are right-censored (event after the last inspection), and \(\ldots\)% are exactly observed.

Code

We fit three models — standard BART, DART, and a Horseshoe Forest — each with \(m = 200\) trees and \(1{,}000\) posterior draws after \(250\) burn-in iterations.

library(ShrinkageTrees)
set.seed(42)

# --- Data generation ---
n <- 100; p <- 500
X <- matrix(rnorm(n * p), n, p)
colnames(X) <- paste0("x", 1:p)
mu_true <- 2 + X[,1] - 0.8*X[,2] + 0.6*X[,3] + 0.5*X[,1]*X[,4] - 0.4*X[,5]
log_T <- mu_true + rnorm(n, sd = 0.5)
T_true <- exp(log_T)

# Interval censoring via inspection times
V <- matrix(runif(n * 3, min = min(T_true) * 0.5, max = max(T_true) * 1.2), n, 3)
V <- t(apply(V, 1, sort))
left_time <- right_time <- rep(NA, n)
for (i in 1:n) {
  inspections <- V[i, ]
  if (T_true[i] <= inspections[1]) {
    left_time[i] <- 0; right_time[i] <- inspections[1]
  } else if (T_true[i] > tail(inspections, 1)) {
    left_time[i] <- tail(inspections, 1); right_time[i] <- Inf
  } else {
    idx <- min(which(inspections >= T_true[i]))
    left_time[i] <- inspections[idx - 1]; right_time[i] <- inspections[idx]
  }
}

# --- Fit models ---
fit_bart <- SurvivalBART(
  y = left_time, X_train = X, outcome_type = "interval-censored",
  right_time = right_time, number_of_trees = 200,
  N_post = 1000, N_burn = 250
)
fit_dart <- SurvivalDART(
  y = left_time, X_train = X, outcome_type = "interval-censored",
  right_time = right_time, number_of_trees = 200,
  N_post = 1000, N_burn = 250
)
fit_horse <- HorseTrees(
  y = left_time, X_train = X, outcome_type = "interval-censored",
  right_time = right_time, number_of_trees = 200, k = 0.1,
  N_post = 1000, N_burn = 250
)

Results

We evaluate each model on a held-out test set of \(n_{\text{test}} = 100\) observations using root mean squared error (RMSE) of the posterior mean of \(\log T\) relative to the true \(\log T\), and 95% credible interval coverage. We repeat the simulation \(\ldots\) times and report averages.

The Horseshoe Forest is expected to outperform standard BART and DART in this sparse setting because the horseshoe prior aggressively shrinks the step heights of trees that split on irrelevant covariates, effectively silencing 495 of the 500 predictors without relying on structural variable selection alone. The \(p \gg n\) regime (\(p = 500\), \(n = 100\)) makes adequate regularisation essential.

5 Package architecture

ShrinkageTrees is available on CRAN and on GitHub at . The package is implemented in R with a C++ backend via Rcpp (Eddelbuettel and François 2011). In this section we describe the design and implementation.

5.1 Fitting functions and S3 classes

The package provides four primary fitting functions and four convenience wrappers for common model configurations. Table ?? lists all eight functions. The primary functions accept arguments that control the outcome type (outcome_type), prior specification (prior_type, local_hp, global_hp), tree structure (number_of_trees, power, base), and MCMC settings (N_post, N_burn, n_chains).

ShrinkageTrees() is the most general prediction model function. It accepts a prior_type argument that selects the leaf prior: "horseshoe" (per-tree global scale), "horseshoe_fw" (forest-wide global scale), "half-cauchy" (local shrinkage only), "standard" (conjugate normal, as in classical BART), or "dirichlet" (Dirichlet splitting, as in DART). HorseTrees() is a convenience wrapper that fixes the prior to the horseshoe and exposes a single tuning parameter \(k\) controlling the half-Cauchy scales via \(\alpha_\tau = \alpha_\lambda = k / \sqrt{m}\). The causal functions CausalShrinkageForest() and CausalHorseForest() mirror this design for the \(\tau\)-learner, with independent prior configurations for the prognostic and treatment effect forests.

All prediction model functions return an object of class "ShrinkageTrees"; all causal model functions return "CausalShrinkageForest". Both classes provide print(), summary(), predict(), and plot() methods. The summary() method reports posterior summaries for \(\sigma\), variable inclusion probabilities, and — for causal models — the ATE with a credible interval. The predict() method re-runs the MCMC sampler on the new covariate matrix using the training data and hyperparameters stored in the fitted object; it always generates posterior samples internally, regardless of whether store_posterior_sample was set during fitting. The returned ShrinkageTreesPrediction object contains posterior means and credible intervals. The plot() method produces MCMC diagnostics (traceplots and posterior densities for \(\sigma\)), variable importance plots, and — for causal models — ATE and CATE visualisations. Plotting uses ggplot2, which is a suggested but not required dependency.

5.2 Rcpp backend

We implement the computationally intensive components of the Gibbs sampler in C++ via Rcpp. This includes the Metropolis-within-Gibbs updates for tree topology (grow, prune, and change moves), the conditional updates for the leaf step heights, the horseshoe augmentation steps for the local and global scale parameters, the Gibbs update for \(\sigma\), and the data augmentation for censored observations. The R layer handles input validation, data preprocessing (centering, scaling, log-transforming survival times), hyperparameter calibration, S3 class construction, and post-processing (back-transformation of predictions).

Internally, the C++ code dispatches between two forest implementations depending on the prior. Priors that require reversible jump proposals ("horseshoe", "horseshoe_fw", "half-cauchy") use a Forest class with paired grow–prune moves that satisfy detailed balance. The "standard" and "dirichlet" priors use a separate StanForest class with birth–death moves, which is simpler and faster when global scale updates are not needed.

The scale mixture prior on the step heights is encapsulated in a C++ class ScaleMixture that follows the strategy pattern. An abstract base class EtaPrior defines a common interface — proposing new local parameters, computing the log prior and proposal densities needed for the Metropolis–Hastings ratio, and optionally performing a global update — while concrete subclasses implement the prior-specific sampling logic. The current subclasses are FixedVariance (conjugate normal, used by BART and DART), HalfCauchy (local shrinkage only), Horseshoe (per-tree global–local shrinkage), and Horseshoe_fw (forest-wide global shrinkage). A factory function selects the appropriate subclass at run time based on the user’s prior_type argument. Because the tree update and reversible jump routines interact with the prior only through the ScaleMixture interface, adding a new global–local shrinkage prior — for example a regularised horseshoe or a Dirichlet–Laplace prior — requires only implementing a new EtaPrior subclass and registering it in the factory, without modifying the sampler logic.

5.3 Multi-chain MCMC

All primary fitting functions accept an n_chains argument. When n_chains > 1, independent chains are dispatched in parallel via parallel::mclapply() using min(n_chains, parallel::detectCores()) cores. After sampling, the posterior draws from all chains are pooled: sample matrices are concatenated row-wise and posterior means are recomputed from the pooled draws. The returned object is a standard ShrinkageTrees or CausalShrinkageForest instance, so all downstream methods work without modification. Per-chain diagnostics (acceptance ratios, \(\sigma\) traces) are retained for convergence assessment.

5.4 Convergence diagnostics via coda

Assessing MCMC convergence is an essential part of any Bayesian workflow, yet most BART implementations leave this task entirely to the user. ShrinkageTrees provides a bridge to the coda ecosystem (Plummer et al. 2006) through an as.mcmc.list() S3 method. This method extracts the posterior draws of \(\sigma\) — split by chain — and wraps each chain in a coda::mcmc object, returning a coda::mcmc.list. Because coda is a suggested rather than required dependency, all functionality remains available when it is not installed. When it is available, summary() automatically reports the effective sample size (ESS) and — for multi-chain fits — the Gelman–Rubin \(\hat{R}\) statistic. Users who require more detailed diagnostics can pass the mcmc.list object directly to any coda function, including geweke.diag(), heidel.diag(), autocorr.plot(), and gelman.plot(), obtaining a complete convergence assessment without custom post-processing code.

6 Related packages

Several R packages implement Bayesian tree ensembles. We review five CRAN packages and compare their capabilities to ShrinkageTrees. Table ?? provides a summary.

BART (Sparapani et al. 2021) is the most comprehensive general-purpose BART package. It supports continuous, binary, and multinomial outcomes, and is, at the time of writing, the only other CRAN package to offer Bayesian tree ensembles for survival data. Survival models include a discrete-time hazard formulation (surv.bart()), an AFT formulation (abart()), competing risks (crisk.bart()), and recurrent events (recur.bart()). The package also supports Dirichlet splitting priors via sparse = TRUE. However, BART does not provide global–local shrinkage priors on the leaf step heights, causal forest models, or interval-censored survival outcomes. Multi-chain MCMC is not natively supported; the mc.* functions parallelise a single chain rather than running independent chains.

dbarts (Dorie 2023) provides a fast, lightweight BART sampler for continuous and binary outcomes. Its key feature is an updatable sampler object: predictors and responses can be modified mid-sampling, which makes dbarts well suited as a conditional model within a larger Gibbs sampler. The package supports multi-chain MCMC but does not implement survival models, \(\tau\)-learner models, or alternative leaf priors.

bcf (Hahn et al. 2020) implements the \(\tau\)-learner with separate prognostic and treatment effect forests for continuous outcomes. It places half-Cauchy and half-Normal hyperpriors on the leaf scales of the two forests, respectively. The package does not support survival endpoints, horseshoe priors, or Dirichlet splitting.

stochtree is a recent package that implements BART, XBART, the \(\tau\)-learner, and XBCF for continuous, binary, and ordinal outcomes. It supports variance forests for heteroskedastic modelling and provides model serialisation for warm-starting. However, stochtree does not implement AFT survival models, Dirichlet splitting priors, or horseshoe shrinkage on the leaf step heights.

SoftBart (Linero and Yang 2018) replaces the hard axis-aligned splits of standard BART with smooth logistic splitting rules, yielding a posterior mean that is a continuous function of the covariates. This can improve performance when the true regression surface is smooth, but introduces additional computational cost and a fundamentally different regularisation mechanism. ShrinkageTrees uses hard decision splits, as in classical BART. Extending the horseshoe and Dirichlet priors to soft splitting rules is a natural direction for future work.

At the time of writing, ShrinkageTrees is the only implementation that combines global–local shrinkage priors on the step heights with Bayesian tree ensembles for survival and causal inference. It is also the only package to support interval-censored survival outcomes within a Bayesian tree ensemble framework and the only package to offer the \(\tau\)-learner for survival endpoints.

7 Discussion and conclusion

We have introduced ShrinkageTrees, an R package for Bayesian tree ensembles that combines global–local shrinkage priors with survival analysis and causal inference. The package decouples structural regularisation from parametric shrinkage, allowing practitioners to tailor the regularisation strategy to the problem at hand. To our knowledge, it is the first CRAN package to offer the \(\tau\)-learner for survival endpoints, horseshoe priors on tree ensemble step heights, and interval-censored survival outcomes within a single framework.

We estimate the average treatment effect via the mixed average treatment effect [MATE; Li et al. (2023)], which averages the posterior CATEs over the observed covariate distribution. The MATE is straightforward to compute but tends to produce overly narrow credible intervals because it conditions on the observed covariates rather than accounting for covariate sampling variability. A similar form of overconfidence is visible in Figure 4, where the credible interval is notably tight. A natural remedy is the Bayesian bootstrap (Rubin 1981), which reweights the covariate distribution with random Dirichlet weights at each MCMC iteration. We leave the integration of the Bayesian bootstrap into ShrinkageTrees as future work.

The package has several limitations that suggest directions for future work. Currently, all covariates must be numeric: continuous, binary, and ordered categorical variables (encoded as integers) are handled naturally by the axis-aligned splitting rules, but unordered categorical variables must be dummy-encoded by the user. This can be suboptimal for high-cardinality factors, where native subset splits — as implemented in bartMachine (Kapelner and Bleich 2016) — may be more efficient. The current AFT formulation assumes normally distributed errors; extending the framework to alternative error distributions (e.g. logistic, log-logistic) would broaden its applicability. The Gibbs sampler can be slow to mix in very high-dimensional settings; incorporating warm-starting or accelerated sampling strategies could improve computational efficiency.

ShrinkageTrees is available on CRAN and actively maintained on GitHub at . The methodological foundations are developed in Jacobs et al. (2025).
