\title{ShrinkageTrees: An R Package for Bayesian Tree Ensembles for Survival Analysis and Causal Inference}


\author{by Tijn Jacobs, Stéphanie van~der~Pas, and Wessel van~Wieringen}

\maketitle

\abstract{%
ShrinkageTrees is an R package for fitting Bayesian tree ensemble models with flexible regularisation mechanisms for survival analysis and causal inference. The package provides unified implementations of BART, DART, and the \(\tau\)-learner (Bayesian Causal Forests) for right-censored and interval-censored survival outcomes via accelerated failure time (AFT) formulations. Multiple regularisation strategies can be combined within a single model: structural sparsity through tree depth priors and Dirichlet splitting priors (DART), and parametric shrinkage on the leaf step heights through global--local scale mixture priors. A key contribution is the implementation of Horseshoe Forests, which place a horseshoe prior directly on the step heights, enabling adaptive shrinkage that aggressively regularises uninformative leaves while preserving strong signals --- a strategy particularly suited to high-dimensional settings. An efficient Rcpp backend and coherent S3 interface support reproducible workflows for both prediction and causal effect estimation.
}

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Bayesian Additive Regression Trees {[}BART; \citet{chipman2010bart}{]} model the response as a sum of \(m\) shallow decision trees, each regularised through a Bayesian prior. BART captures complex interactions and non-linearities without specifying a parametric form. This makes BART well suited to settings where the relationship between covariates and the response is unknown, including survival analysis. The \CRANpkg{BART} package \citep{sparapani2021nonparametric} is, to our knowledge, the only CRAN package that implements Bayesian tree ensembles for survival data. It supports discrete-time hazard and AFT formulations, competing risks, and recurrent events. However, it does not support interval-censored outcomes, causal inference models, or global--local shrinkage priors on the leaf parameters. We address all three limitations in \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}.

BART has become a popular tool for causal inference \citep{hill2011bayesian, dorie2019automated}. \citet{hahn2020bayesian} propose Bayesian Causal Forests (BCF), which decompose the response surface into a prognostic function and a treatment effect function; \citet{caron2022estimating} later term this the \(\tau\)-learner. This separation improves estimation of heterogeneous treatment effects. The \CRANpkg{bcf} and \CRANpkg{stochtree} packages implement the \(\tau\)-learner for continuous outcomes. Causal inference and survival analysis arise together naturally in clinical trials and observational studies, where the outcome is a time-to-event and treatment effects may vary across patient subgroups. No existing R package implements the \(\tau\)-learner for survival endpoints.

High-dimensional settings, where the number of predictors \(p\) is large relative to the sample size \(n\), demand adequate regularisation. We distinguish two components on which regularisation can act in Bayesian tree ensembles. The first is the tree structure, which determines how the covariate space is partitioned. The second is the leaf parameters, which determine each partition's contribution to the fitted function. Standard BART regularises both components generically: a depth prior discourages complex trees, and a conjugate normal prior shrinks the leaf parameters. DART \citep{linero2018dart} strengthens structural regularisation by placing a Dirichlet prior on the splitting probabilities, encouraging the model to split on a smaller subset of relevant predictors. \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe} target the second component and place global--local shrinkage priors, such as the horseshoe \citep{carvalho2010horseshoe}, directly on the leaf parameters. This shrinks weak or spurious contributions aggressively toward zero while preserving strong signals. We refer to tree ensembles with such priors as \emph{Horseshoe Forests}.

We introduce \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}, a unified R package that addresses the three
gaps identified above. We extend BART to survival outcomes via AFT models for both
right-censored and interval-censored data. We extend the \(\tau\)-learner to survival endpoints,
providing, to our knowledge, the first causal forest implementation for time-to-event
outcomes in R. We implement both Dirichlet splitting priors and Horseshoe Forests,
allowing both forms of regularisation to be combined freely within a single model.
The Horseshoe Forest sampler relies on an efficient reversible jump algorithm
implemented in C++ via \CRANpkg{Rcpp} \citep{eddelbuettel2011rcpp}, and is designed to
be extendable to other global--local shrinkage priors. We provide a coherent S3
interface with \code{print}, \code{summary}, \code{predict}, and \code{plot} methods. \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe} develop the methodological foundations.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 provides a brief overview of the statistical methodology. Section 3 reviews related R packages. Section 4 describes the package architecture. Section 5 demonstrates the package on a high-dimensional ovarian cancer survival dataset. Section 6 concludes.

\section{Overview of Statistical Methodology}\label{overview-of-statistical-methodology}

We build on three components. First, we review the BART model and highlight the distinction between its two parameter groups: tree structures and step heights. Second, we show how regularisation targets either group --- structural sparsity via tree priors and Dirichlet splitting, parametric shrinkage via global--local priors on the step heights --- and how both strategies can be combined. Third, we extend the framework to causal inference via the \(\tau\)-learner and to survival outcomes via accelerated failure time models.

\subsection{Bayesian Additive Regression Trees}\label{bayesian-additive-regression-trees}

We adapt the BART model of \citet{chipman2010bart}. BART models the response as a sum of \(m\) shallow decision trees:
\[
Y_i = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).
\]
Each tree \(j\) is characterised by two parameter groups. The \emph{tree structure} \(\mathcal{T}_j\) encodes the topology and the splitting rules: which variables to split on and at what thresholds. It determines how the covariate space is partitioned into disjoint regions. The \emph{step heights} \(\mathcal{H}_j = \{h_{j1}, \ldots, h_{jL_j}\}\) are the values assigned to the \(L_j\) leaves and determine each region's contribution to the fitted function. For input \(\mathbf{x}\), the function \(g(\mathbf{x};\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j)\) returns the step height of the leaf that \(\mathbf{x}\) reaches (see Figure \ref{fig:ExampleTree}). This distinction between \(\mathcal{T}\) and \(\mathcal{H}\) is central to the regularisation framework we develop below.

We specify independent priors on \(\mathcal{T}_j\) and \(\mathcal{H}_j\):
\[
p(\mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) = p_{\mathcal{T}}(\mathcal{T}_j)\, p_{\mathcal{H}}(\mathcal{H}_j \mid \mathcal{T}_j) \quad \text{for } j = 1,\ldots,m.
\]
The tree structure prior \(p_{\mathcal{T}}\) is a branching process \citep{chipman1998bayesiancart, rockova2020theory} in which a node at depth \(d\) splits with probability \(\alpha\, (1 + d)^{-\beta}\). At each internal node, the splitting variable is drawn uniformly from the available covariates and the split point uniformly from the observed range.

The step height prior \(p_{\mathcal{H}}\) assigns independent Gaussian priors conditional on the tree structure:
\[
h_{j\ell} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \frac{1}{4\sqrt{m}}\right),
\]
where the variance is scaled so that the prior places approximately 95\% of its mass within the observed range of the response. This prior is conjugate, so the Gibbs sampler can update the step heights efficiently.

We model the error variance as \(\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-}\chi^2(\nu,\, \psi)\), with \(\nu = 3\) and \(\psi\) chosen so that the prior is centred around a rough estimate of \(\sigma^2\) \citep{chipman2010bart}.

\subsection{Regularisation}\label{regularisation}

The parameters of a BART ensemble fall into two groups: tree structure parameters (nodes, splitting variables, split points) that determine how the covariate space is partitioned, and step heights that determine each partition's contribution to the fitted function. Regularisation can target either group.

\textbf{Structural regularisation.} Standard BART regularises the tree structure through the branching process prior of \citet{chipman2010bart}: each internal node at depth \(d\) splits with probability \(\alpha (1 + d)^{-\beta}\), where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) and \(\beta \geq 0\) discourage deep trees. Under the default values \(\alpha = 0.95\) and \(\beta = 2\), most trees remain shallow. DART \citep{linero2018dart} strengthens structural regularisation by replacing the uniform distribution over splitting variables with a Dirichlet prior on the splitting probabilities \(\mathbf{s} = (s_1, \ldots, s_p)\). By concentrating the prior on sparse probability vectors, DART encourages the ensemble to split on a subset of relevant variables, achieving structural variable selection.

\textbf{Shrinkage on step heights.} Rather than regularising the tree structure alone, we can place shrinkage priors directly on the leaf step heights. \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} adopts a scale mixture of normals representation,
\[
h_\ell \mid \lambda_\ell, \tau, \omega \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \omega\, \lambda_\ell^2\, \tau^2),
\]
where \(\tau\) is a global shrinkage parameter shared across all leaves, \(\lambda_\ell\) is a local scale specific to leaf \(\ell\), and \(\omega\) is a fixed scaling constant. The global parameter \(\tau\) controls the overall degree of regularisation, while the local parameters \(\lambda_\ell\) allow individual leaves with strong signals to escape shrinkage.

The horseshoe prior \citep{carvalho2010horseshoe} arises as a specific instantiation of this framework by placing independent half-Cauchy priors on both the local and global scales:
\[
\lambda_\ell \sim \text{C}^+(0, \alpha_\lambda), \qquad
\tau \sim \text{C}^+(0, \alpha_\tau),
\]
where \(\text{C}^+(0, \alpha)\) denotes the half-Cauchy distribution with scale \(\alpha\). The half-Cauchy has its median at \(\alpha\) and possesses heavy tails --- its mean and variance do not exist --- which allows the local scales to take very large values when the data support a strong effect \citep{polson2012halfcauchy}. This combination aggressively shrinks the contributions of uninformative leaves towards zero while preserving large, data-supported signals. Following \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}, we set \(\alpha_\tau = \alpha_\lambda = k / \sqrt{m}\). The Dirichlet and horseshoe priors address complementary aspects of regularisation --- structural sparsity and parametric shrinkage, respectively --- and can be combined within a single model in \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}.

\subsection{Prediction and causal models}\label{prediction-and-causal-models}

The BART formulation above estimates a single function of the covariates --- we refer to this as the \emph{prediction model}. The prediction models can themselves be used for causal inference: including the treatment indicator as a covariate yields an S-learner, while fitting separate models per treatment arm yields a T-learner \citep{caron2022estimating}. However, the dedicated approach in \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} is the \(\tau\)-learner, the decomposition proposed by \citet{hahn2020bayesian} as Bayesian Causal Forests (BCF) and later termed the \(\tau\)-learner by \citet{caron2022estimating}. Given a binary treatment indicator \(A_i \in \{0, 1\}\) and an estimate of the propensity score \(\hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \hat{P}(A_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)\), the \emph{causal model} writes
\[
y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)) + A_i\, \tau(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i,
\]
where \(\mu\) is a prognostic function modelled by a forest of \(m_\mu\) trees and \(\tau\) is a treatment effect function modelled by a separate forest of \(m_\tau\) trees. This separation allows the prognostic and treatment effect components to have different regularisation and complexity. The conditional average treatment effect is \(\text{CATE}(\mathbf{x}) = \tau(\mathbf{x})\), and the average treatment effect is \(\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[\tau(\mathbf{X})]\).

More generally, the scalar multiplier on \(\tau\) in the \(\tau\)-learner need not equal the raw treatment indicator. Writing \(b_i\) for the coding applied to subject \(i\), the model becomes \(y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)) + b_i\, \tau(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i\). \citet{hahn2020bayesian} discuss several choices. \emph{Centered} coding sets \(b_i = A_i - 1/2\), which is the original \(\tau\)-learner parameterisation and the default in \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}. \emph{Binary} coding uses \(b_i = A_i\). \emph{Adaptive} coding sets \(b_i = A_i - \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)\), incorporating the estimated propensity score directly into the treatment multiplier; this is the parameterisation adopted in the \CRANpkg{bcf} package. Finally, the \emph{invariant} (parameter-expanded) coding of \citet[Section 5.2]{hahn2020bayesian} assigns group-specific intercepts \(b_0, b_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/2)\) and writes \(y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i) + b_{A_i}\, \tilde{\tau}(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i\), where \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) are updated within the Gibbs sampler via conjugate normal updates and the treatment effect is recovered as \(\tau(\mathbf{x}_i) = (b_1 - b_0)\, \tilde{\tau}(\mathbf{x}_i)\). This parameterisation is invariant to the labelling of treatment and control. All four options are available in \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} via the \texttt{treatment\_coding} argument.

For survival outcomes, we adopt an accelerated failure time (AFT) formulation. Let \(T_i\) denote the event time, \(C_i\) the censoring time, \(Y_i = \min(T_i, C_i)\) the observed follow-up time, and \(\delta_i \in \{0, 1\}\) the event indicator. The prediction model becomes
\[
\log T_i = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, T_j, H_j) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).
\]
Right-censored observations (\(\delta_i = 0\)) are handled by imputing \(\log T_i\) from a truncated normal distribution within the Gibbs sampler. \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} also supports interval-censored outcomes, where the event time is known only to lie in an interval \((l_i, r_i]\); these are imputed analogously. The \(\tau\)-learner extends directly to the AFT setting by replacing \(y_i\) with \(\log T_i\).

\subsection{Posterior computation}\label{posterior-computation}

All models are fitted using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The outer loop is a Gibbs sampler that cycles through updates of the tree topologies, the leaf step heights, the error variance \(\sigma^2\), and --- where applicable --- the shrinkage parameters and censored event times. Trees are updated one at a time via Bayesian backfitting \citep{chipman2010bart}: at each sweep, a partial residual is formed by subtracting the contributions of all other trees from the response, and tree \(j\) is then updated conditional on the current state of all other trees. The tree topology is modified through Metropolis--Hastings proposals that either grow a terminal node into an internal node with two new leaves, or prune an internal node back to a leaf. Each proposal is accepted or rejected according to the standard Metropolis--Hastings ratio, which balances the likelihood improvement against the prior on tree structure.

Under the standard BART and Dirichlet (DART) priors, where the leaf parameters have a conjugate normal prior, the marginal likelihood of a proposed tree can be computed in closed form and the step heights are integrated out analytically. This yields the birth--death sampler described in \citet{chipman2010bart}; a detailed algorithmic exposition is given in \citet{kapelner2016bartmachine}. When global--local shrinkage priors such as the horseshoe are placed on the step heights, this conjugacy is lost: the local and global scale parameters \(\lambda_\ell\) and \(\tau\) must be sampled explicitly, and the tree proposals become reversible jump MCMC moves that account for the change in the number of scale parameters when a leaf is added or removed. The details of this reversible jump construction, including the proposal distributions and acceptance ratios, are given in \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}.

\section{Related Packages}\label{related-packages}

Several R packages implement Bayesian tree ensembles. We review five CRAN packages and compare their capabilities to \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}. Table \ref{tab:pkg-comparison} provides a summary.

\CRANpkg{BART} \citep{sparapani2021nonparametric} is the most comprehensive general-purpose BART package. It supports continuous, binary, and multinomial outcomes, and is, at the time of writing, the only other CRAN package to offer Bayesian tree ensembles for survival data. Survival models include a discrete-time hazard formulation (\texttt{surv.bart()}), an AFT formulation (\texttt{abart()}), competing risks (\texttt{crisk.bart()}), and recurrent events (\texttt{recur.bart()}). The package also supports Dirichlet splitting priors via \texttt{sparse\ =\ TRUE}. However, \CRANpkg{BART} does not provide global--local shrinkage priors on the leaf step heights, causal forest models, or interval-censored survival outcomes. Multi-chain MCMC is not natively supported; the \texttt{mc.*} functions parallelise a single chain rather than running independent chains.

\CRANpkg{dbarts} \citep{dorie2023dbarts} provides a fast, lightweight BART sampler for continuous and binary outcomes. Its key feature is an updatable sampler object: predictors and responses can be modified mid-sampling, which makes \CRANpkg{dbarts} well suited as a conditional model within a larger Gibbs sampler. The package supports multi-chain MCMC but does not implement survival models, \(\tau\)-learner models, or alternative leaf priors.

\CRANpkg{bcf} \citep{hahn2020bayesian} implements the \(\tau\)-learner with separate prognostic and treatment effect forests for continuous outcomes. It places half-Cauchy and half-Normal hyperpriors on the leaf scales of the two forests, respectively. The package does not support survival endpoints, horseshoe priors, or Dirichlet splitting.

\CRANpkg{stochtree} is a recent package that implements BART, XBART, the \(\tau\)-learner, and XBCF for continuous, binary, and ordinal outcomes. It supports variance forests for heteroskedastic modelling and provides model serialisation for warm-starting. However, \CRANpkg{stochtree} does not implement AFT survival models, Dirichlet splitting priors, or horseshoe shrinkage on the leaf step heights.

\CRANpkg{SoftBart} \citep{linero2018softbart} replaces the hard axis-aligned splits of standard BART with smooth logistic splitting rules, yielding a posterior mean that is a continuous function of the covariates. This can improve performance when the true regression surface is smooth, but introduces additional computational cost and a fundamentally different regularisation mechanism. \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} uses hard decision splits, as in classical BART. Extending the horseshoe and Dirichlet priors to soft splitting rules is a natural direction for future work.

At the time of writing, \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} is the only implementation that combines global--local shrinkage priors on the step heights with Bayesian tree ensembles for survival and causal inference. It is also the only package to support interval-censored survival outcomes within a Bayesian tree ensemble framework and the only package to offer the \(\tau\)-learner for survival endpoints.

\section{Package Architecture}\label{package-architecture}

\CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} is available on CRAN and on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/tijn-jacobs/ShrinkageTrees}. The package is implemented in R with a C++ backend via \CRANpkg{Rcpp} \citep{eddelbuettel2011rcpp}. In this section we describe the design and implementation.

\texttt{ShrinkageTrees()} is the most general prediction model function. It accepts a \texttt{prior\_type} argument that selects the leaf prior: \texttt{"horseshoe"} (per-tree global scale), \texttt{"horseshoe\_fw"} (forest-wide global scale), \texttt{"half-cauchy"} (local shrinkage only), \texttt{"standard"} (conjugate normal, as in classical BART), or \texttt{"dirichlet"} (Dirichlet splitting, as in DART). \texttt{HorseTrees()} is a convenience wrapper that fixes the prior to the horseshoe and exposes a single tuning parameter \(k\) controlling the half-Cauchy scales via \(\alpha_\tau = \alpha_\lambda = k / \sqrt{m}\). The causal functions \texttt{CausalShrinkageForest()} and \texttt{CausalHorseForest()} mirror this design for the \(\tau\)-learner, with independent prior configurations for the prognostic and treatment effect forests.

All prediction model functions return an object of class \texttt{"ShrinkageTrees"}; all causal model functions return \texttt{"CausalShrinkageForest"}. Both classes provide \texttt{print()}, \texttt{summary()}, \texttt{predict()}, and \texttt{plot()} methods. The \texttt{summary()} method reports posterior summaries for \(\sigma\), variable inclusion probabilities, and --- for causal models --- the ATE with a credible interval. The \texttt{predict()} method re-runs the MCMC sampler on the new covariate matrix using the training data and hyperparameters stored in the fitted object; it always generates posterior samples internally, regardless of whether \texttt{store\_posterior\_sample} was set during fitting. The returned \texttt{ShrinkageTreesPrediction} object contains posterior means and credible intervals. The \texttt{plot()} method produces MCMC diagnostics (traceplots and posterior densities for \(\sigma\)), variable importance plots, and --- for causal models --- ATE and CATE visualisations. Plotting uses \CRANpkg{ggplot2}, which is a suggested but not required dependency.

\subsection{Rcpp backend}\label{rcpp-backend}

We implement the computationally intensive components of the Gibbs sampler in C++ via \CRANpkg{Rcpp}. This includes the Metropolis-within-Gibbs updates for tree topology (grow, prune, and change moves), the conditional updates for the leaf step heights, the horseshoe augmentation steps for the local and global scale parameters, the Gibbs update for \(\sigma\), and the data augmentation for censored observations. The R layer handles input validation, data preprocessing (centering, scaling, log-transforming survival times), hyperparameter calibration, S3 class construction, and post-processing (back-transformation of predictions).

Internally, the C++ code dispatches between two forest implementations depending on the prior. Priors that require reversible jump proposals (\texttt{"horseshoe"}, \texttt{"horseshoe\_fw"}, \texttt{"half-cauchy"}) use a \texttt{Forest} class with paired grow--prune moves that satisfy detailed balance. The \texttt{"standard"} and \texttt{"dirichlet"} priors use a separate \texttt{StanForest} class with birth--death moves, which is simpler and faster when global scale updates are not needed.

The scale mixture prior on the step heights is encapsulated in a C++ class \texttt{ScaleMixture} that follows the strategy pattern. An abstract base class \texttt{EtaPrior} defines a common interface --- proposing new local parameters, computing the log prior and proposal densities needed for the Metropolis--Hastings ratio, and optionally performing a global update --- while concrete subclasses implement the prior-specific sampling logic. The current subclasses are \texttt{FixedVariance} (conjugate normal, used by BART and DART), \texttt{HalfCauchy} (local shrinkage only), \texttt{Horseshoe} (per-tree global--local shrinkage), and \texttt{Horseshoe\_fw} (forest-wide global shrinkage). A factory function selects the appropriate subclass at run time based on the user's \texttt{prior\_type} argument. Because the tree update and reversible jump routines interact with the prior only through the \texttt{ScaleMixture} interface, adding a new global--local shrinkage prior --- for example a regularised horseshoe or a Dirichlet--Laplace prior --- requires only implementing a new \texttt{EtaPrior} subclass and registering it in the factory, without modifying the sampler logic.

\subsection{Multi-chain MCMC}\label{multi-chain-mcmc}

All primary fitting functions accept an \texttt{n\_chains} argument. When \texttt{n\_chains\ \textgreater{}\ 1}, independent chains are dispatched in parallel via \texttt{parallel::mclapply()} using \texttt{min(n\_chains,\ parallel::detectCores())} cores. After sampling, the posterior draws from all chains are pooled: sample matrices are concatenated row-wise and posterior means are recomputed from the pooled draws. The returned object is a standard \texttt{ShrinkageTrees} or \texttt{CausalShrinkageForest} instance, so all downstream methods work without modification. Per-chain diagnostics (acceptance ratios, \(\sigma\) traces) are retained for convergence assessment.

\subsection{Convergence diagnostics via coda}\label{convergence-diagnostics-via-coda}

Assessing MCMC convergence is an essential part of any Bayesian workflow, yet most BART implementations leave this task entirely to the user. \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} provides a bridge to the \CRANpkg{coda} ecosystem \citep{plummer2006coda} through an \texttt{as.mcmc.list()} S3 method. This method extracts the posterior draws of \(\sigma\) --- split by chain --- and wraps each chain in a \texttt{coda::mcmc} object, returning a \texttt{coda::mcmc.list}. Because \pkg{coda} is a suggested rather than required dependency, all functionality remains available when it is not installed. When it is available, \texttt{summary()} automatically reports the effective sample size (ESS) and --- for multi-chain fits --- the Gelman--Rubin \(\hat{R}\) statistic. Users who require more detailed diagnostics can pass the \texttt{mcmc.list} object directly to any \pkg{coda} function, including \texttt{geweke.diag()}, \texttt{heidel.diag()}, \texttt{autocorr.plot()}, and \texttt{gelman.plot()}, obtaining a complete convergence assessment without custom post-processing code.

We note that XBART \citep{he2019xbart} uses a fundamentally different fitting strategy based on stochastic tree search rather than the standard Metropolis--Hastings back-fitting algorithm, so a direct wall-clock comparison would not be informative; we therefore exclude it from the benchmark.

\textbf{Scaling behaviour.} Figure\textasciitilde{}\ref{fig:scaling} shows how wall-clock time scales with \(n\), \(p\), and \(m\) individually. Time scales approximately linearly in \(m\) and \(n\), and sub-linearly in \(p\) because only a subset of covariates is evaluated at each splitting rule proposal.

\section{Application: Ovarian Cancer Survival}\label{application-ovarian-cancer-survival}

We demonstrate the package on the \texttt{ovarian} dataset shipped with
\CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}. This dataset is curated from The Cancer Genome Atlas ovarian
cancer cohort {[}TCGA-OV; \citet{tcga2011ovarian}{]} and contains overall survival times, a
binary treatment indicator, clinical covariates, and expression levels of 2,000 genes
selected by median absolute deviation. Ovarian cancer is one of the most lethal
gynaecological malignancies, accounting for approximately 85\% of ovarian cancer
deaths in its high-grade serous form \citep{tcga2011ovarian}. The five-year survival rate
is below 50\%. First-line treatment involves platinum-based chemotherapy, and gene
expression patterns are known to be associated with survival duration and chemotherapy
response \citep{tcga2011ovarian}. The choice between carboplatin and cisplatin --- two
platinum agents with different toxicity profiles --- remains a central clinical question.
The dataset provides a challenging high-dimensional causal survival problem, with
gene expression data yielding \(p \gg n\), that exercises the main features of the
package. We first fit a prediction model for survival, then extend the analysis to
causal inference and estimate the heterogeneous treatment effect.

\begin{verbatim}
library(ShrinkageTrees)
set.seed(42)
data("ovarian")

clin      <- ovarian$clinical
time      <- clin$OS_time / 30.44   # convert days to months
status    <- clin$OS_event
treatment <- clin$treatment

X <- cbind(age        = clin$age,
           figo_stage = clin$figo_stage,
           tumor_grade = clin$tumor_grade,
           ovarian$X)
\end{verbatim}

The dataset comprises 357 patients with 2,000 gene expression features and three clinical covariates (age, FIGO stage, tumour grade). We hold out 20\% of the patients for evaluating predictive accuracy.

\begin{verbatim}
train_idx <- sample(seq_len(nrow(clin)), size = floor(0.8 * nrow(clin)))
test_idx  <- setdiff(seq_len(nrow(clin)), train_idx)

X_train <- X[train_idx, ];  X_test <- X[test_idx, ]
time_train <- time[train_idx];  time_test <- time[test_idx]
status_train <- status[train_idx];  status_test <- status[test_idx]
\end{verbatim}

We fit a Horseshoe Forest via \texttt{HorseTrees()} using an AFT formulation with right-censoring and four independent MCMC chains. The horseshoe prior applies global--local shrinkage to the leaf step heights, providing regularisation in this high-dimensional setting where the number of predictors far exceeds the sample size.

\begin{verbatim}
fit_horse <- HorseTrees(
  y               = time_train,
  status          = status_train,
  X_train         = X_train,
  X_test          = X_test,
  outcome_type    = "right-censored",
  timescale       = "time",
  number_of_trees = 200,
  k               = 0.1,
  N_post          = 5000,
  N_burn          = 5000,
  n_chains        = 4
)
\end{verbatim}

We now estimate heterogeneous treatment effects of carboplatin versus cisplatin using \texttt{CausalHorseForest()}, which fits a \(\tau\)-learner with horseshoe shrinkage on both the prognostic and treatment effect forests. We first estimate propensity scores using a probit Horseshoe Forest on the clinical covariates. We choose a Horseshoe Forest over logistic regression because it handles potential non-linearities and interactions between clinical covariates without manual specification, while the horseshoe prior guards against overfitting in this low-sample-size setting. Using the full gene expression matrix for propensity estimation would risk overfitting given \(p \gg n\); clinical covariates alone capture the main sources of confounding by indication.

  y               = treatment,
  X_train         = X_ps,
  outcome_type    = "binary",
  number_of_trees = 200,
  k               = 0.1,
  N_post          = 2000,
  N_burn          = 2000
)
propensity <- ps_fit$train_predictions
X_control  <- cbind(propensity = propensity, X)
\end{verbatim}

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}\label{discussion-and-conclusion}

We have introduced \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}, an R package for Bayesian tree ensembles that combines global--local shrinkage priors with survival analysis and causal inference. The package decouples structural regularisation from parametric shrinkage, allowing practitioners to tailor the regularisation strategy to the problem at hand. To our knowledge, it is the first CRAN package to offer the \(\tau\)-learner for survival endpoints, horseshoe priors on tree ensemble step heights, and interval-censored survival outcomes within a single framework.

We estimate the average treatment effect via the mixed average treatment effect {[}MATE; \citet{li2023bayesian}{]}, which averages the posterior CATEs over the observed covariate distribution. The MATE is straightforward to compute but tends to produce overly narrow credible intervals because it conditions on the observed covariates rather than accounting for covariate sampling variability. A similar form of overconfidence is visible in Figure \ref{fig:treatment-effects-fig}, where the credible interval is notably tight. A natural remedy is the Bayesian bootstrap \citep{rubin1981bayesian}, which reweights the covariate distribution with random Dirichlet weights at each MCMC iteration. We leave the integration of the Bayesian bootstrap into \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} as future work.

The package has several limitations that suggest directions for future work. Currently, all covariates must be numeric: continuous, binary, and ordered categorical variables (encoded as integers) are handled naturally by the axis-aligned splitting rules, but unordered categorical variables must be dummy-encoded by the user. This can be suboptimal for high-cardinality factors, where native subset splits --- as implemented in \CRANpkg{bartMachine} \citep{kapelner2016bartmachine} --- may be more efficient. The current AFT formulation assumes normally distributed errors; extending the framework to alternative error distributions (e.g.~logistic, log-logistic) would broaden its applicability. The Gibbs sampler can be slow to mix in very high-dimensional settings; incorporating warm-starting or accelerated sampling strategies could improve computational efficiency.

\CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} is available on CRAN and actively maintained on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/tijn-jacobs/ShrinkageTrees}. The methodological foundations are developed in \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}.
