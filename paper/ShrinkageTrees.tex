\title{ShrinkageTrees: An R Package for Bayesian Tree Ensembles for Survival Analysis and Causal Inference}


\author{by Tijn Jacobs, Stéphanie van~der~Pas, and Wessel van~Wieringen}

\maketitle

\abstract{%
ShrinkageTrees is an R package for fitting Bayesian tree ensemble models with flexible regularisation mechanisms for survival analysis and causal inference. The package provides unified implementations of BART, DART, and Bayesian Causal Forests (BCF) for right-censored and interval-censored survival outcomes via accelerated failure time (AFT) formulations. Multiple regularisation strategies can be combined within a single model: structural sparsity through tree depth priors and Dirichlet splitting priors (DART), and parametric shrinkage on the leaf step heights through global--local scale mixture priors. A key contribution is the implementation of Horseshoe Forests, which place a horseshoe prior directly on the step heights, enabling adaptive shrinkage that aggressively regularises uninformative leaves while preserving strong signals --- a strategy particularly suited to high-dimensional settings. An efficient Rcpp backend and coherent S3 interface support reproducible workflows for both prediction and causal effect estimation.
}

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Bayesian Additive Regression Trees {[}BART; \citet{chipman2010bart}{]} are a widely adopted non-parametric method for flexible regression and prediction. BART models the response as a sum of \(m\) shallow decision trees, each regularised through a Bayesian prior, and can capture complex interactions and non-linearities without requiring the user to specify a parametric form. These properties make BART attractive for the analysis of time-to-event data, where the relationship between covariates and survival is often unknown. \citet{sparapani2021nonparametric} extend BART to survival outcomes through both a discrete-time hazard formulation and an accelerated failure time (AFT) approach within the \CRANpkg{BART} package. The AFT formulation models \(\log T_i\) as a sum of trees plus a noise term, and accommodates right-censored observations through data augmentation in the Gibbs sampler. To date, the \CRANpkg{BART} package is, to our knowledge, the only CRAN package that implements Bayesian tree ensembles for survival data. It lacks support for several recent methodological developments that we discuss below.

Beyond prediction, there is growing interest in using tree ensembles for causal inference. Bayesian Causal Forests {[}BCF; \citet{hahn2020bayesian}{]} decompose the response surface into a prognostic function \(\mu(\mathbf{x})\), shared across treatment groups, and a treatment effect function \(\tau(\mathbf{x})\) that captures heterogeneity in the causal effect. This two-forest structure improves estimation of conditional average treatment effects (CATEs) compared to single-forest approaches. The combination of causal inference and survival analysis arises naturally in clinical trials and observational studies, where the outcome is a time-to-event and treatment effects may vary across patient subgroups. Yet, at the time of writing, no existing R package provides Bayesian causal forests for survival endpoints.

In high-dimensional settings, where the number of candidate predictors \(p\) is large relative to the sample size \(n\), regularisation becomes critical. Standard BART controls complexity through a prior on tree depth and a conjugate normal prior on the leaf step heights \(h_{j\ell}\). This does not explicitly distinguish between two different aspects of regularisation: \emph{structural sparsity} --- which variables enter the model and how deep trees grow --- and \emph{parametric shrinkage} --- how strongly individual leaf contributions are pulled towards zero. DART \citep{linero2018dart} addresses structural sparsity by placing a Dirichlet prior on the splitting probabilities, encouraging the model to concentrate splits on relevant variables. \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe} take a complementary approach and apply global--local shrinkage priors, such as the horseshoe \citep{carvalho2010horseshoe}, directly to the leaf step heights. The horseshoe prior aggressively shrinks uninformative contributions towards zero while leaving large, well-supported effects essentially unpenalised. We refer to tree ensembles equipped with this prior as \emph{Horseshoe Forests}. In \pkg{ShrinkageTrees}, structural and parametric regularisation can be combined freely within a single model, giving the practitioner fine-grained control over the regularisation strategy.

We introduce the \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} package, a unified R implementation that brings together these developments. The package supports survival outcomes via AFT models with both right-censoring and interval-censoring; causal inference through BCF with separate prognostic and treatment effect forests, extended to survival endpoints; flexible regularisation by decoupling structural sparsity from parametric shrinkage; and multi-chain MCMC with parallel execution. We implement the core samplers in C++ via \CRANpkg{Rcpp} \citep{eddelbuettel2011rcpp} and provide a coherent S3 interface with \texttt{print}, \texttt{summary}, \texttt{predict}, and \texttt{plot} methods. The methodological foundations are developed in \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 provides a brief overview of the statistical methodology. Section 3 reviews related R packages and compares their capabilities. Section 4 describes the package architecture and implementation. Section 5 demonstrates the package on a high-dimensional ovarian cancer survival dataset. Section 6 concludes with a discussion.

\section{Overview of Statistical Methodology}\label{overview-of-statistical-methodology}

\subsection{Bayesian Additive Regression Trees}\label{bayesian-additive-regression-trees}

We adapt the Bayesian Additive Regression Trees (BART) model, originally introduced by \citet{chipman2010bart} to estimate the conditional mean function \(\mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i]\) in regression settings with continuous uncensored outcomes. The model assumes
\[
Y_i = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2),
\]
where each \(\mathcal{T}_j\) is a binary decision tree that defines recursive partitioning rules over the covariate space, and \(\mathcal{H}_j = \{h_{j1}, \ldots, h_{jL_j}\}\) denotes the set of step heights associated with the \(L_j\) leaves of tree \(\mathcal{T}_j\).

For a given input \(\mathbf{x}\), the function \(g(\mathbf{x};\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j)\) maps \(\mathbf{x}\) to a leaf node \(\ell\) in \(\mathcal{T}_j\) and returns the corresponding value \(h_{j\ell}\). Each tree partitions the covariate space into disjoint regions, assigning a constant prediction to each (see Figure \ref{fig:ExampleTree}). The prediction of the \(j\)-th tree for individual \(i\) is obtained by
\[
g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, \mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) = \sum_{\ell=1}^{L_j} h_{j\ell} \cdot \mathbb{1}(\mathbf{x}_i;\, \mathcal{T}_j, \ell),
\]
where \(\mathbb{1}(\mathbf{x}_i;\, \mathcal{T}_j, \ell) = 1\) if \(\mathbf{x}_i\) is assigned to leaf \(\ell\) in tree \(\mathcal{T}_j\), and zero otherwise.

A prior is specified over the ensemble \(\{(\mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j)\}_{j=1}^m\) by independently assigning a prior on the tree structure and a conditional prior on the step heights given the tree:
\[
p(\mathcal{T}_j, \mathcal{H}_j) = p_{\mathcal{T}}(\mathcal{T}_j)\, p_{\mathcal{H}}(\mathcal{H}_j \mid \mathcal{T}_j) \quad \text{for } j = 1,\ldots,m.
\]
These define the two key components of the BART prior. The tree structure \(\mathcal{T}\) of each tree is drawn from a prior \(p_{\mathcal{T}}(\mathcal{T})\) originally described by \citet{chipman1998bayesiancart}, based on a heterogeneous Galton--Watson branching process \citep{rockova2020theory}. The process begins at a root node and recursively decides whether to split a node based on its depth \(d\). The probability that a node at depth \(d\) is non-terminal is given by
\[
p(d) = \alpha\, (1 + d)^{-\beta},
\]
with hyperparameters \(\alpha > 0\) and \(\beta > 0\) governing the expected depth and shape of the tree. Given a tree topology, each interior node is assigned a binary decision rule of the form \([x_\rho \leq c]\), where the splitting variable \(\rho\) is selected uniformly from the available covariates and the split point \(c\) is drawn uniformly from the range of observed values for variable \(\rho\). The splitting rules are constrained to those producing valid partitions, that is, each subset contains a minimal chosen number of observations.

The prior \(p_{\mathcal{H}}\) on the step heights \(\mathcal{H}\) is defined conditionally on the tree structure \(\mathcal{T}\). The step heights \(h_{j\ell}\) are assigned independent Gaussian priors of the form
\[
h_{j\ell} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \frac{1}{4\sqrt{m}}\right),
\]
where the variance is scaled to ensure that the prior places approximately 95\% of its mass within the observed range of the response. This prior is conjugate, enabling efficient non-reversible updates using a Gibbs sampler.

The noise is modelled as \(\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\), with the error variance \(\sigma^2\) assigned a scaled inverse-chi-squared prior:
\[
\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-}\chi^2(\nu,\, \psi),
\]
where \(\nu\) denotes the degrees of freedom parameter and \(\psi\) the prior scale. We follow \citet{chipman2010bart}, setting \(\nu = 3\) and choosing \(\psi\) so that the prior is centred around a rough estimate of \(\sigma^2\).

\subsection{Regularisation}\label{regularisation}

We distinguish three regularisation mechanisms, each targeting a different aspect of the model.

\textbf{Tree structure priors.} Standard BART regularisation follows the branching process prior of \citet{chipman2010bart}: each internal node at depth \(d\) splits with probability \(\alpha (1 + d)^{-\beta}\), where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) and \(\beta \geq 0\) discourage deep trees. Under the default values \(\alpha = 0.95\) and \(\beta = 2\), most trees remain shallow.

\textbf{Dirichlet splitting priors.} DART \citep{linero2018dart} replaces the uniform distribution over splitting variables with a Dirichlet prior on the splitting probabilities \(\mathbf{s} = (s_1, \ldots, s_p)\). By concentrating the prior on sparse probability vectors, DART encourages the ensemble to split on a subset of relevant variables, achieving structural variable selection.

\textbf{Global--local shrinkage priors on step heights.} Rather than regularising the tree structure alone, one can place shrinkage priors directly on the leaf step heights. \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} adopts a scale mixture of normals representation,
\[
h_\ell \mid \lambda_\ell, \tau, \omega \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \omega\, \lambda_\ell^2\, \tau^2),
\]
where \(\tau\) is a global shrinkage parameter shared across all leaves, \(\lambda_\ell\) is a local scale specific to leaf \(\ell\), and \(\omega\) is a fixed scaling constant. The global parameter \(\tau\) controls the overall degree of regularisation, while the local parameters \(\lambda_\ell\) allow individual leaves with strong signals to escape shrinkage.

The horseshoe prior \citep{carvalho2010horseshoe} arises as a specific instantiation of this framework by placing independent half-Cauchy priors on both the local and global scales:
\[
\lambda_\ell \sim \text{C}^+(0, \alpha_\lambda), \qquad
\tau \sim \text{C}^+(0, \alpha_\tau),
\]
where \(\text{C}^+(0, \alpha)\) denotes the half-Cauchy distribution with scale \(\alpha\). The half-Cauchy has its median at \(\alpha\) and possesses heavy tails --- its mean and variance do not exist --- which allows the local scales to take very large values when the data support a strong effect \citep{polson2012halfcauchy}. This combination aggressively shrinks the contributions of uninformative leaves towards zero while preserving large, data-supported signals. Following \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}, we set \(\alpha_\tau = \alpha_\lambda = k / \sqrt{m}\). The Dirichlet and horseshoe priors address complementary aspects of regularisation --- structural sparsity and parametric shrinkage, respectively --- and can be combined within a single model in \pkg{ShrinkageTrees}.

\subsection{Prediction and causal models}\label{prediction-and-causal-models}

The BART formulation above estimates a single function of the covariates --- we refer to this as the \emph{prediction model}. For causal inference, we adopt the Bayesian Causal Forest {[}BCF; \citet{hahn2020bayesian}{]} decomposition, also known as the \(\tau\)-learner \citep{caron2022estimating}. Given a binary treatment indicator \(A_i \in \{0, 1\}\) and an estimate of the propensity score \(\hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \hat{P}(A_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)\), the \emph{causal model} writes
\[
y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)) + A_i\, \tau(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i,
\]
where \(\mu\) is a prognostic function modelled by a forest of \(m_\mu\) trees and \(\tau\) is a treatment effect function modelled by a separate forest of \(m_\tau\) trees. This separation allows the prognostic and treatment effect components to have different regularisation and complexity. The conditional average treatment effect is \(\text{CATE}(\mathbf{x}) = \tau(\mathbf{x})\), and the average treatment effect is \(\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[\tau(\mathbf{X})]\).

More generally, the scalar multiplier on \(\tau\) in the BCF decomposition need not equal the raw treatment indicator. Writing \(b_i\) for the coding applied to subject \(i\), the model becomes \(y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)) + b_i\, \tau(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i\). \citet{hahn2020bayesian} discuss several choices. \emph{Centered} coding sets \(b_i = A_i - 1/2\), which is the original BCF parameterisation and the default in \pkg{ShrinkageTrees}. \emph{Binary} coding uses \(b_i = A_i\). \emph{Adaptive} coding sets \(b_i = A_i - \hat{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)\), incorporating the estimated propensity score directly into the treatment multiplier; this is the parameterisation adopted in the \CRANpkg{bcf} package. Finally, the \emph{invariant} (parameter-expanded) coding of \citet[Section 5.2]{hahn2020bayesian} assigns group-specific intercepts \(b_0, b_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/2)\) and writes \(y_i = \mu(\mathbf{x}_i) + b_{A_i}\, \tilde{\tau}(\mathbf{x}_i) + \varepsilon_i\), where \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) are updated within the Gibbs sampler via conjugate normal updates and the treatment effect is recovered as \(\tau(\mathbf{x}_i) = (b_1 - b_0)\, \tilde{\tau}(\mathbf{x}_i)\). This parameterisation is invariant to the labelling of treatment and control. All four options are available in \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} via the \texttt{treatment\_coding} argument.

For survival outcomes, we adopt an accelerated failure time (AFT) formulation. Let \(T_i\) denote the event time, \(C_i\) the censoring time, \(Y_i = \min(T_i, C_i)\) the observed follow-up time, and \(\delta_i \in \{0, 1\}\) the event indicator. The prediction model becomes
\[
\log T_i = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\mathbf{x}_i;\, T_j, H_j) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).
\]
Right-censored observations (\(\delta_i = 0\)) are handled by imputing \(\log T_i\) from a truncated normal distribution within the Gibbs sampler. \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} also supports interval-censored outcomes, where the event time is known only to lie in an interval \((l_i, r_i]\); these are imputed analogously. The causal model extends directly to the AFT setting by replacing \(y_i\) with \(\log T_i\).

\subsection{Posterior computation}\label{posterior-computation}

All models are fitted using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The outer loop is a Gibbs sampler that cycles through updates of the tree topologies, the leaf step heights, the error variance \(\sigma^2\), and --- where applicable --- the shrinkage parameters and censored event times. Trees are updated one at a time via Bayesian backfitting \citep{chipman2010bart}: at each sweep, a partial residual is formed by subtracting the contributions of all other trees from the response, and tree \(j\) is then updated conditional on the current state of all other trees. The tree topology is modified through Metropolis--Hastings proposals that either grow a terminal node into an internal node with two new leaves, or prune an internal node back to a leaf. Each proposal is accepted or rejected according to the standard Metropolis--Hastings ratio, which balances the likelihood improvement against the prior on tree structure.

Under the standard BART and Dirichlet (DART) priors, where the leaf parameters have a conjugate normal prior, the marginal likelihood of a proposed tree can be computed in closed form and the step heights are integrated out analytically. This yields the birth--death sampler described in \citet{chipman2010bart}; a detailed algorithmic exposition is given in \citet{kapelner2016bartmachine}. When global--local shrinkage priors such as the horseshoe are placed on the step heights, this conjugacy is lost: the local and global scale parameters \(\lambda_\ell\) and \(\tau\) must be sampled explicitly, and the tree proposals become reversible jump MCMC moves that account for the change in the number of scale parameters when a leaf is added or removed. The details of this reversible jump construction, including the proposal distributions and acceptance ratios, are given in \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}.

\section{Related Packages}\label{related-packages}

Several R packages implement Bayesian tree ensembles. We review five CRAN packages and compare their capabilities to \pkg{ShrinkageTrees}. Table \ref{tab:pkg-comparison} provides a summary.

\CRANpkg{BART} \citep{sparapani2021nonparametric} is the most comprehensive general-purpose BART package. It supports continuous, binary, and multinomial outcomes, and is, at the time of writing, the only other CRAN package to offer Bayesian tree ensembles for survival data. Survival models include a discrete-time hazard formulation (\texttt{surv.bart()}), an AFT formulation (\texttt{abart()}), competing risks (\texttt{crisk.bart()}), and recurrent events (\texttt{recur.bart()}). The package also supports Dirichlet splitting priors via \texttt{sparse\ =\ TRUE}. However, \CRANpkg{BART} does not provide global--local shrinkage priors on the leaf step heights, causal forest models, or interval-censored survival outcomes. Multi-chain MCMC is not natively supported; the \texttt{mc.*} functions parallelise a single chain rather than running independent chains.

\CRANpkg{dbarts} \citep{dorie2023dbarts} provides a fast, lightweight BART sampler for continuous and binary outcomes. Its key feature is an updatable sampler object: predictors and responses can be modified mid-sampling, which makes \CRANpkg{dbarts} well suited as a conditional model within a larger Gibbs sampler. The package supports multi-chain MCMC but does not implement survival models, causal forests, or alternative leaf priors.

\CRANpkg{bcf} \citep{hahn2020bayesian} implements Bayesian Causal Forests with separate prognostic and treatment effect forests for continuous outcomes. It places half-Cauchy and half-Normal hyperpriors on the leaf scales of the two forests, respectively. The package does not support survival endpoints, horseshoe priors, or Dirichlet splitting.

\CRANpkg{stochtree} is a recent package that implements BART, XBART, BCF, and XBCF for continuous, binary, and ordinal outcomes. It supports variance forests for heteroskedastic modelling and provides model serialisation for warm-starting. However, \CRANpkg{stochtree} does not implement AFT survival models, Dirichlet splitting priors, or horseshoe shrinkage on the leaf step heights.

\CRANpkg{SoftBart} \citep{linero2018softbart} replaces the hard axis-aligned splits of standard BART with smooth logistic splitting rules, yielding a posterior mean that is a continuous function of the covariates. This can improve performance when the true regression surface is smooth, but introduces additional computational cost and a fundamentally different regularisation mechanism. \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} exclusively uses hard decision splits, as in classical BART; extending the horseshoe and Dirichlet priors to soft splitting rules is an interesting direction for future work but is beyond the scope of this package.

At the time of writing, \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} is the only implementation that combines global--local shrinkage priors on the step heights with Bayesian tree ensembles for survival and causal inference. It is also the only package to support interval-censored survival outcomes within a Bayesian tree ensemble framework and the only package to offer causal forests for survival endpoints.

\section{Package Architecture}\label{package-architecture}

\CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} is available on CRAN and on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/tijn-jacobs/ShrinkageTrees}. The package is implemented in R with a C++ backend via \CRANpkg{Rcpp} \citep{eddelbuettel2011rcpp}. In this section we describe the design and implementation.

\subsection{Fitting functions and S3 classes}\label{fitting-functions-and-s3-classes}

The package provides four primary fitting functions and four convenience wrappers for common model configurations. Table \ref{tab:fitting-functions} lists all eight functions. The primary functions accept arguments that control the outcome type (\texttt{outcome\_type}), prior specification (\texttt{prior\_type}, \texttt{local\_hp}, \texttt{global\_hp}), tree structure (\texttt{number\_of\_trees}, \texttt{power}, \texttt{base}), and MCMC settings (\texttt{N\_post}, \texttt{N\_burn}, \texttt{n\_chains}).

\texttt{ShrinkageTrees()} is the most general prediction model function. It accepts a \texttt{prior\_type} argument that selects the leaf prior: \texttt{"horseshoe"} (per-tree global scale), \texttt{"horseshoe\_fw"} (forest-wide global scale), \texttt{"half-cauchy"} (local shrinkage only), \texttt{"standard"} (conjugate normal, as in classical BART), or \texttt{"dirichlet"} (Dirichlet splitting, as in DART). \texttt{HorseTrees()} is a convenience wrapper that fixes the prior to the horseshoe and exposes a single tuning parameter \(k\) controlling the half-Cauchy scales via \(\alpha_\tau = \alpha_\lambda = k / \sqrt{m}\). The causal functions \texttt{CausalShrinkageForest()} and \texttt{CausalHorseForest()} mirror this design for the causal BCF formulation, with independent prior configurations for the prognostic and treatment effect forests.

All prediction model functions return an object of class \texttt{"ShrinkageTrees"}; all causal model functions return \texttt{"CausalShrinkageForest"}. Both classes provide \texttt{print()}, \texttt{summary()}, \texttt{predict()}, and \texttt{plot()} methods. The \texttt{summary()} method reports posterior summaries for \(\sigma\), variable inclusion probabilities, and --- for causal models --- the ATE with a credible interval. The \texttt{predict()} method re-runs the MCMC sampler on the new covariate matrix using the training data and hyperparameters stored in the fitted object; it always generates posterior samples internally, regardless of whether \texttt{store\_posterior\_sample} was set during fitting. The returned \texttt{ShrinkageTreesPrediction} object contains posterior means and credible intervals. The \texttt{plot()} method produces MCMC diagnostics (traceplots and posterior densities for \(\sigma\)), variable importance plots, and --- for causal models --- ATE and CATE visualisations. Plotting uses \CRANpkg{ggplot2}, which is a suggested but not required dependency.

\subsection{Rcpp backend}\label{rcpp-backend}

We implement the computationally intensive components of the Gibbs sampler in C++ via \CRANpkg{Rcpp}. This includes the Metropolis-within-Gibbs updates for tree topology (grow, prune, and change moves), the conditional updates for the leaf step heights, the horseshoe augmentation steps for the local and global scale parameters, the Gibbs update for \(\sigma\), and the data augmentation for censored observations. The R layer handles input validation, data preprocessing (centering, scaling, log-transforming survival times), hyperparameter calibration, S3 class construction, and post-processing (back-transformation of predictions).

Internally, the C++ code dispatches between two forest implementations depending on the prior. Priors that require reversible jump proposals (\texttt{"horseshoe"}, \texttt{"horseshoe\_fw"}, \texttt{"half-cauchy"}) use a \texttt{Forest} class with paired grow--prune moves that satisfy detailed balance. The \texttt{"standard"} and \texttt{"dirichlet"} priors use a separate \texttt{StanForest} class with birth--death moves, which is simpler and faster when global scale updates are not needed.

The scale mixture prior on the step heights is encapsulated in a C++ class \texttt{ScaleMixture} that follows the strategy pattern. An abstract base class \texttt{EtaPrior} defines a common interface --- proposing new local parameters, computing the log prior and proposal densities needed for the Metropolis--Hastings ratio, and optionally performing a global update --- while concrete subclasses implement the prior-specific sampling logic. The current subclasses are \texttt{FixedVariance} (conjugate normal, used by BART and DART), \texttt{HalfCauchy} (local shrinkage only), \texttt{Horseshoe} (per-tree global--local shrinkage), and \texttt{Horseshoe\_fw} (forest-wide global shrinkage). A factory function selects the appropriate subclass at run time based on the user's \texttt{prior\_type} argument. Because the tree update and reversible jump routines interact with the prior only through the \texttt{ScaleMixture} interface, adding a new global--local shrinkage prior --- for example a regularised horseshoe or a Dirichlet--Laplace prior --- requires only implementing a new \texttt{EtaPrior} subclass and registering it in the factory, without modifying the sampler logic.

\subsection{Multi-chain MCMC}\label{multi-chain-mcmc}

All primary fitting functions accept an \texttt{n\_chains} argument. When \texttt{n\_chains\ \textgreater{}\ 1}, independent chains are dispatched in parallel via \texttt{parallel::mclapply()} using \texttt{min(n\_chains,\ parallel::detectCores())} cores. After sampling, the posterior draws from all chains are pooled: sample matrices are concatenated row-wise and posterior means are recomputed from the pooled draws. The returned object is a standard \texttt{ShrinkageTrees} or \texttt{CausalShrinkageForest} instance, so all downstream methods work without modification. Per-chain diagnostics (acceptance ratios, \(\sigma\) traces) are retained for convergence assessment.

\subsection{Convergence diagnostics via coda}\label{convergence-diagnostics-via-coda}

Assessing MCMC convergence is an essential part of any Bayesian workflow, yet most BART implementations leave this task entirely to the user. \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} provides a bridge to the \CRANpkg{coda} ecosystem \citep{plummer2006coda} through an \texttt{as.mcmc.list()} S3 method. This method extracts the posterior draws of \(\sigma\) --- split by chain --- and wraps each chain in a \texttt{coda::mcmc} object, returning a \texttt{coda::mcmc.list}. Because \pkg{coda} is a suggested rather than required dependency, all functionality remains available when it is not installed. When it is available, \texttt{summary()} automatically reports the effective sample size (ESS) and --- for multi-chain fits --- the Gelman--Rubin \(\hat{R}\) statistic. Users who require more detailed diagnostics can pass the \texttt{mcmc.list} object directly to any \pkg{coda} function, including \texttt{geweke.diag()}, \texttt{heidel.diag()}, \texttt{autocorr.plot()}, and \texttt{gelman.plot()}, obtaining a complete convergence assessment without custom post-processing code.

\section{Application: Ovarian Cancer Survival}\label{application-ovarian-cancer-survival}

We demonstrate the package on the \texttt{ovarian} dataset shipped with \pkg{ShrinkageTrees}. This dataset is curated from The Cancer Genome Atlas ovarian cancer cohort {[}TCGA-OV; \citet{tcga2011ovarian}{]} and contains overall survival times, a binary treatment indicator, clinical covariates, and expression levels of 2,000 genes selected by median absolute deviation. Ovarian cancer is one of the most lethal gynaecological malignancies, with a five-year survival rate below 50\%. First-line treatment typically involves platinum-based chemotherapy, and the choice between carboplatin and cisplatin --- two agents with different toxicity profiles --- remains a central clinical question. The dataset provides a high-dimensional causal survival problem that exercises the main features of the package. We first fit prediction models for survival, then extend the analysis to causal inference using the BCF formulation.

\subsection{Data and setup}\label{data-and-setup}

\begin{verbatim}
library(ShrinkageTrees)
data("ovarian")

clin      <- ovarian$clinical
time      <- clin$OS_time
status    <- clin$OS_event
treatment <- clin$treatment

X <- cbind(age        = clin$age,
           figo_stage = clin$figo_stage,
           tumor_grade = clin$tumor_grade,
           ovarian$X)

cat("n =", nrow(clin), "| p =", ncol(X),
    "| event rate =", round(mean(status), 2),
    "| carboplatin =", sum(treatment), "/ cisplatin =", sum(1 - treatment), "\n")
\end{verbatim}

The dataset comprises 357 patients with 2,000 gene expression features and three clinical covariates (age, FIGO stage, tumour grade). We hold out 20\% of the patients for evaluating predictive accuracy.

\begin{verbatim}
set.seed(2025)
train_idx <- sample(seq_len(nrow(clin)), size = floor(0.8 * nrow(clin)))
test_idx  <- setdiff(seq_len(nrow(clin)), train_idx)

X_train <- X[train_idx, ];  X_test <- X[test_idx, ]
time_train <- time[train_idx];  time_test <- time[test_idx]
status_train <- status[train_idx];  status_test <- status[test_idx]
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Survival prediction models}\label{survival-prediction-models}

We fit two prediction models on the training data: a standard BART model via \texttt{SurvivalBART()} and a Horseshoe Forest via \texttt{HorseTrees()}. Both use an AFT formulation with right-censoring and two independent MCMC chains. The BART model serves as a baseline; the Horseshoe Forest applies global--local shrinkage to the leaf step heights, which is expected to improve regularisation in this high-dimensional setting where the number of predictors far exceeds the sample size.

\begin{verbatim}
fit_bart <- SurvivalBART(
  time            = time_train,
  status          = status_train,
  X_train         = X_train,
  X_test          = X_test,
  number_of_trees = 50,
  N_post          = 5000,
  N_burn          = 5000,
  n_chains        = 2,
  store_posterior_sample = TRUE,
  verbose         = FALSE
)
\end{verbatim}

\begin{verbatim}
fit_horse <- HorseTrees(
  y               = time_train,
  status          = status_train,
  X_train         = X_train,
  X_test          = X_test,
  outcome_type    = "right-censored",
  timescale       = "time",
  number_of_trees = 50,
  k               = 0.1,
  N_post          = 5000,
  N_burn          = 5000,
  n_chains        = 2,
  store_posterior_sample = TRUE,
  verbose         = FALSE
)
\end{verbatim}

The \texttt{print()} and \texttt{summary()} methods provide a concise overview of the fitted model, including the outcome type, MCMC settings, acceptance ratio, and posterior summaries for \(\sigma\).

\begin{verbatim}
print(fit_horse)
summary(fit_horse)
\end{verbatim}

We evaluate predictive accuracy on the held-out test set using the concordance index {[}C-index; \citet{harrell1996concordance}{]}, where a value of 1 indicates perfect discrimination and 0.5 corresponds to random prediction.

\begin{verbatim}
library(survival)
c_bart  <- concordance(Surv(time_test, status_test) ~ fit_bart$test_predictions)
c_horse <- concordance(Surv(time_test, status_test) ~ fit_horse$test_predictions)

cat("Test C-index — SurvivalBART:", round(c_bart$concordance, 3),
    " HorseTrees:", round(c_horse$concordance, 3), "\n")
\end{verbatim}

Both models achieve a test C-index of approximately 0.60, indicating modest but above-chance discrimination --- a reasonable result given the extremely high-dimensional setting (\(p = 2{,}003 \gg n = 357\)). The Horseshoe Forest and standard BART perform comparably here, consistent with the horseshoe prior providing regularisation benefits primarily in estimation and uncertainty quantification rather than in point prediction summaries.

\subsection{Causal survival analysis}\label{causal-survival-analysis}

We now estimate heterogeneous treatment effects of carboplatin versus cisplatin using \texttt{CausalHorseForest()}, which fits a BCF model with horseshoe shrinkage on both the prognostic and treatment effect forests. We first estimate propensity scores from a logistic regression on the clinical covariates. Using the full gene expression matrix for propensity estimation would risk overfitting given \(p \gg n\); clinical covariates alone capture the main sources of confounding by indication.

\begin{verbatim}
ps_model   <- glm(treatment ~ age + factor(figo_stage) + factor(tumor_grade),
                   family = binomial, data = clin)
propensity <- predict(ps_model, type = "response")
X_control  <- cbind(propensity = propensity, X)
\end{verbatim}

\begin{verbatim}
set.seed(2025)
fit_causal <- CausalHorseForest(
  y                         = log(time),
  status                    = status,
  X_train_control           = X_control,
  X_train_treat             = X,
  treatment_indicator_train = treatment,
  outcome_type              = "right-censored",
  timescale                 = "log",
  number_of_trees           = 50,
  k                         = 0.1,
  N_post                    = 5000,
  N_burn                    = 5000,
  n_chains                  = 2,
  store_posterior_sample    = TRUE,
  verbose                   = FALSE
)
summary(fit_causal)
\end{verbatim}

The \texttt{summary()} method reports the posterior average treatment effect (ATE) on the log-survival scale with a 95\% credible interval. Here we pass \texttt{log(time)} directly with \texttt{timescale\ =\ "log"}, so all treatment effect estimates remain on the log-survival scale. A positive ATE indicates that carboplatin is associated with longer survival times.

\subsection{Interval-censored survival}\label{interval-censored-survival}

Although the ovarian dataset contains right-censored observations, \pkg{ShrinkageTrees} also supports interval-censored outcomes via \texttt{outcome\_type\ =\ "interval-censored"} with \texttt{left\_time} and \texttt{right\_time} arguments following the convention of \texttt{survival::Surv(type\ =\ "interval2")}. See \texttt{?ShrinkageTrees} for details and examples.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}\label{discussion-and-conclusion}

We have introduced \CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees}, an R package for Bayesian tree ensembles that combines global--local shrinkage priors with survival analysis and causal inference. The package decouples structural regularisation from parametric shrinkage, allowing practitioners to tailor the regularisation strategy to the problem at hand. To our knowledge, it is the first CRAN package to offer Bayesian causal forests for survival endpoints, horseshoe priors on tree ensemble step heights, and interval-censored survival outcomes within a single framework.

The package has several limitations that suggest directions for future work. Currently, all covariates must be numeric: continuous, binary, and ordered categorical variables (encoded as integers) are handled naturally by the axis-aligned splitting rules, but unordered categorical variables must be dummy-encoded by the user. This can be suboptimal for high-cardinality factors, where native subset splits --- as implemented in \CRANpkg{bartMachine} \citep{kapelner2016bartmachine} --- may be more efficient. The current AFT formulation assumes normally distributed errors; extending the framework to alternative error distributions (e.g.~logistic, log-logistic) would broaden its applicability. The Gibbs sampler can be slow to mix in very high-dimensional settings; incorporating warm-starting or accelerated sampling strategies could improve computational efficiency.

\CRANpkg{ShrinkageTrees} is available on CRAN and actively maintained on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/tijn-jacobs/ShrinkageTrees}. The methodological foundations are developed in \citet{jacobs2025horseshoe}.
